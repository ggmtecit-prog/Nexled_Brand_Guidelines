Color Palette

Green leads the NexLed identity, support colors add controlled contrast, and neutrals keep dense technical content clean and readable.

Build recognition first, variation second

The palette is functional, not decorative. Green establishes the brand immediately, blue and red support specific moments of contrast, and the neutral range protects clarity across technical layouts, catalog content, and interface surfaces.

When a page starts feeling too colorful, reduce instead of adding more. NexLed should read as controlled, professional, and brand-led rather than visually noisy.

Core Brand Family

Green System

The green family comes directly from the NexLed mark and should carry the brand wherever recognition matters first. It is the default family for identity, primary emphasis, key headings, and leading actions.

  • •

    Use green-primary where the brand needs maximum presence and immediate recognition.

  • •

    Use green-secondary to extend the family in softer emphasis areas without changing the brand lead.

  • •

    Use the lighter green tones for soft surfaces, contextual highlights, or supportive backgrounds, not as replacements for the primary mark color.

Application Rules

Apply the palette with discipline

Preferred

Lead with green

  • •

    Use green for the mark, key headings, primary actions, and the strongest emphasis moments.

  • •

    Let lighter greens support highlights and soft brand backgrounds.

Controlled Use

Support with blue and red

  • •

    Use blue or red only when they clarify a category, comparison, or secondary emphasis.

  • •

    Keep them subordinate to the green system in any brand-facing composition.

Reserved

Keep interaction accents isolated

This accent belongs to system-defined visited or interaction states only. It is not part of the brand palette for layouts, surfaces, or identity blocks.

Avoid
  • •

    Do not build a page where blue or red becomes the dominant brand color.

  • •

    Do not introduce additional hues outside the published system.

  • •

    Do not use the reserved interaction accent in logos, headings, or large surfaces.

  • •

    Do not rely on light neutrals where contrast or legibility would be reduced.
